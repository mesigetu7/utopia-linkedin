Street furniture tells you a lot about how a city thinks about its own future.

Benches, planters, bollards, signage bases. These are the things people touch every day. They're also the things cities consistently underinvest in — and then spend repeatedly replacing.

Drive through any newly developed corridor in Addis. Look at the condition of the benches two rainy seasons in. The paint is gone. The surface is cracked. Some are already leaning. And the budget to replace them was never allocated because everyone assumed the originals would last.

This is the pattern: design for installation, not for a decade.

GFRC changes what's possible for public furniture. Cast in molds, so every piece can have a distinctive shape tied to its neighborhood or corridor. Weather-resistant enough to survive Ethiopian summers and rainy seasons without degrading. Heavy enough to stay in place, light enough to be moved if needed.

And the mold cost is a one-time investment. Every additional piece is cheaper than the first.

Public space is infrastructure. It should be designed to last as long as the road beneath it.
