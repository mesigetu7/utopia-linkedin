Most construction decisions in Ethiopia are made by the person holding the budget, not the person who understands the material.

The architect specifies something. The engineer approves it. The developer looks at the line item and substitutes it for something cheaper. By the time it reaches the contractor, the original intent is barely recognizable.

This is not unique to Ethiopia. But it has a specific consequence here: the buildings that get built are systematically less durable, less distinctive, and less representative of what was designed than they should be.

The substitution happens because the decision-maker doesn't have a clear way to evaluate "cheaper now vs. cheaper over time." So they default to cheaper now, every time.

The argument for UHPC and GFRC has to be made to the person with the budget, not just the person with the pen. That's the real sales process — helping a developer understand that a material that costs more per square meter but lasts twice as long without maintenance is not a premium. It's a discount on a longer timeline.

Who do you think holds the most real decision-making power on a construction project — the architect, the developer, or the contractor?
