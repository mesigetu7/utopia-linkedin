The first question is almost always the same.

"How much does it cost?"

Not "what can it do?" Not "how long does it last?" Not "what does it look like?" Just — how much.

And I get it. Budget is real. Materials are bought on spreadsheets.

But here's what I've noticed: the clients who lead with cost almost always end up spending more. They choose the cheaper option, it fails or fades in two years, and they replace it. The math never works out the way they planned.

The clients who lead with "what can this do for my project" — those are the ones who build something that still looks right a decade later.

Cost is a real question. But it's the wrong first question. The first question should be: what do I actually want this element to do, and for how long?

UHPC and GFRC are not cheap compared to conventional alternatives. They are, however, significantly cheaper than replacing conventional alternatives every few years in Ethiopia's climate.

That's the conversation I want to be having.

What's the calculation you use when you're deciding between materials on a project?
